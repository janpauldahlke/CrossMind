## **We read your ideas. Here's the hard talk.**

## We read every application. Most of what came in was not bad \- it was *weak*. "AI-powered automation for job applications." "A voice tutor for language learning." "An AI app to generate YouTube Shorts." "A hyper-personalized AI assistant." These ideas might have been fundable three years ago. Today, dozens of well-funded companies already do each of these. An angel looking at your pitch will ask: why does this need to exist when \[established competitor\] already owns this space?

## Almost every weak idea has a strong version hiding inside it. You usually do not need a new idea. You need a sharper buyer, a sharper wedge, a sharper "why now," and one piece of real evidence that a human being other than your co-founder wants this.

## 

## **How to find the right idea**

## The best startup ideas are not invented from nothing. They come from the intersection of what you know, what you have lived, and what is changing right now.

###  **Start with yourself**

* What did you study? What field do you work in? What industry do you know from the inside?

* What tools or workflows frustrate you or your colleagues every week?

* What problems have you watched real people struggle with \- not read about, but seen up close?

* What changed recently that makes this possible or urgent now: AI capability, regulation, access, cost, distribution, buyer urgency?

* Who can you text *today* to check whether this pain is real?

* If you have a team: what is each person good at? Where do your combined strengths overlap with a real market?

Quick filter: if your idea is a social media app, an AI therapist, or a generic dating app, you are competing with thousands of identical pitches. The most common ideas are the least fundable. If the answer is "everyone could use this," narrow it until you can name the first 10 likely users.

### 

### **Look outward \- see what's actually launching**

### Spend 30 minutes checking where people are already launching, complaining, buying, or hacking together workarounds. The point is not to copy what is trending \- it is to avoid building the 40th generic version of something, and to find a sharper wedge.

* [X: "introducing"](https://x.com/search?q=introducing&src=typed_query) \- startups announce launches on X. Scroll for 10 minutes and you'll see what's being built right now, what's crowded, and where the gaps are. Scroll down a bit for the gems.

* [Hacker News: Show HN](https://news.ycombinator.com/show) \- real builders posting real projects. The comments tell you what's strong and weak faster than any investor.

* [Product Hunt](https://www.producthunt.com/) \- daily launches ranked by votes. Good for seeing what's crowded and what gets attention.

* [Indie Hackers](https://www.indiehackers.com) \- real founders, real revenue, real failures, zero VC polish.

* [TrustMRR](https://trustmrr.com/) \- startups sharing verified revenue publicly. See what people actually pay for, not just what gets upvotes.

* [r/startups](https://www.reddit.com/r/startups/) and [r/SaaS](https://www.reddit.com/r/SaaS/) \- founders sharing what works and what doesn't. Raw signal, no PR filter.

* [Contrary Research](https://research.contrary.com) \- free independent deep-dives on private markets and sectors.

After the scan, write one sentence: **what is crowded, what is still painful, and what edge could we have?**

**The wrong way:** sit down, brainstorm for 10 minutes, and go with the first thing that sounds cool. That's how weak ideas get born \- millions of people have the same surface-level thoughts.

**The right way:** start from a problem you have personally experienced or observed, in a domain where you carry knowledge others do not have, and check whether the timing is right.

## 

## **The 7 weak-idea patterns we kept seeing**

1. **"ChatGPT for \[industry\]"** \- a thin wrapper over a foundation model with no proprietary data, workflow, or distribution. The model is the moat, and you do not own the model. *Reframe:* own a specific workflow inside a specific buyer's day; AI is the mechanism, not the product.

2. **"AI \[job title\]"** \- "AI lawyer," "AI recruiter," "AI accountant," pitched without naming a single human whose job actually changes. Replacing a job is a 10-year arc; replacing a *task* inside that job is a 10-week sale. *Reframe:* name the task, the tool it replaces, and the hour of the week it gets done.

3. **Feature, not company** \- one clever workflow that Notion, Linear, HubSpot, or Microsoft ships as a checkbox in 6 months. *Reframe:* show the wedge that grows into a system of record, not a single button.

4. **Big number, no plan** \- "the global X market is $50B." Nobody buys a market; people buy software. Paul Graham: *"Live in the future, then build what's missing."* Not: live in McKinsey decks. *Reframe:* describe your first 10 customers by name, city, and pain.

5. **Solution looking for a problem** \- "we have a cool agent framework, looking for a vertical." The inverse of how durable companies start. *Reframe:* start from a pain you have personally watched a real person curse at this month.

6. **Generic horizontal "for everyone"** \- "an AI assistant for SMBs," "a copilot for knowledge workers." "Everyone" is code for "no one in particular, and no distribution plan." *Reframe:* pick one beachhead narrow enough to list every prospect on one screen.

7. **Second-mover with no edge** \- "like Harvey, but cheaper" / "like Clay, but for DACH." Cheaper is not a strategy. *Reframe:* find the thing the category leader structurally cannot do \- a workflow, a data source, or a buyer they refuse to serve.

## **What "strong" looks like \- three before/after reframings**

## These are illustrations of how to sharpen an idea \- not claims about exact market size, pricing, or what investors will fund.

## YC's Gustaf Alströmer: *"We're excited about AI-native companies that don't sell software \- they sell the service. Instead of giving you a tool, they just do the work."* That's the bar now: not a copilot, but a replacement for the service itself.

## **1\. SaaS challenger \- kill a legacy product** *Weak:* "An AI-powered project management tool." *Strong:* "Construction companies in Germany pay €800/seat/month for Procore to manage job sites. The software was designed in 2002 and bolted on features for 20 years. We rebuilt the core workflow \- daily logs, RFIs, punch lists \- from scratch with AI. Our system auto-generates the daily log from site photos and voice notes. No data entry. €80/seat. Why now: AI collapsed the cost of building software by 10–100x (Jared Friedman, YC). The millions of lines of code that protected Procore are no longer a moat." *Why this works:* construction software is a €15B market dominated by players who haven't rethought a single workflow since 2002\. The wedge is one workflow (daily logs), not the whole platform. The open risk: enterprise sales cycles run 6–12 months. The smart move is to start with subcontractors (5–20 person firms) who can switch in a week.

## **2\. Software for agents \- not humans** *Weak:* "An AI agent platform for businesses." *Strong:* "Every CRM, ERP, and HRIS was built for humans clicking buttons in a browser. AI agents are already managing pipelines, updating records, and triggering workflows \- but they do it by clicking the same buttons through brittle browser automation. We rebuild the CRM interface as an agent-native API layer: MCP endpoints, structured documentation, and programmatic access agents can discover and use without a human in the loop. Why now: the next trillion users on the internet are not people \- they are agents (Aaron Epstein, YC). And they need software built for them." *Why this works:* everyone is building agents. Almost nobody is building the software those agents need. The wedge: pick one legacy tool (a specific CRM, HRIS, or ERP) and make it agent-accessible. The open risk: incumbents will add API layers too. The defense is speed and depth \- your agent-native interface works better because it was built for agents from day one.

## **3\. AI-native service \- replace the service, not the tool** *Weak:* "An AI copilot that automates accounting tasks for SMBs." *Strong:* "We are the bookkeeper. A D2C founder doing €1.2M on Shopify pays a part-time Steuerberater €400/month and still spends 4 hours fighting Lexoffice before every filing deadline. We connect to Shopify, the bank feed, and DATEV, auto-classify every transaction into SKR03, and export a clean file the Steuerberater signs off on without corrections. €99/month. Why now: Germany's e-invoicing mandate (ZUGFeRD/XRechnung) forced B2B transactions into structured formats from January 2025\. For the first time, incoming invoices are machine-readable." *Why this works:* total spend on services is many times larger than spend on software. This pitch replaces the bookkeeper, not the Steuerberater \- who carries legal liability an AI tool does not. So it works as a layer that makes the Steuerberater faster. The wedge is transaction classification, not full accounting.

## The pattern: name a specific person in a specific moment of pain, start with the narrowest wedge someone would pay for this week, and be honest about the risk that could kill the idea. Every strong pitch knows its weakest point.

## **Reality check \- 7 questions to answer before June 6**

## Answer each in under 30 seconds. Honestly. If you can't answer more than two, go back to the whiteboard.

1. **Wedge** \- Can I describe what we do in one sentence without "platform," "ecosystem," or "AI-powered"?

2. **Buyer** \- Can I name the exact human who signs the invoice \- their role, company size, and country?

3. **Demand** \- Would someone be genuinely upset if this disappeared tomorrow? Not "interested" \- *upset*. Waitlists, signups, and "that's cool" are not demand. Money is demand. Panic when it breaks is demand.

4. **Status quo** \- What are people doing right now to solve this, even badly? If the answer is "nothing," the problem probably isn't painful enough. Your real competitor is not another startup \- it's the spreadsheet-and-email workaround your buyer already lives with.

5. **Why now** \- Is there something that was impossible or illegal in 2023 that makes this work in 2026? A new API, a regulation, a cost collapse?

6. **Founder-market fit** \- Have I personally lived this pain, or do I have an unfair relationship with people who have?

7. **Evidence** \- Has a stranger said "take my money" \- or at least "when can I try it" \- in writing, this month? Compliments are not evidence. Rob Fitzpatrick (*The Mom Test*): *"Compliments are the fool's gold of customer learning."*

## **Stress-test your idea with AI before June 6**

## Paste your idea into ChatGPT, Claude, or Cursor. Then copy these questions and ask the AI to walk you through them one at a time. Do not let it be nice to you \- tell it to push back on every answer.

1. *"What is the strongest evidence that someone actually wants this \- not 'is interested,' but would be genuinely upset if it disappeared tomorrow?"*

2. *"What are people doing right now to solve this problem, even badly? What does that workaround cost them in hours or money?"*

3. *"Name the actual human who needs this most. What is their title? What gets them promoted? What gets them fired?"*

4. *"What is the smallest version of this that someone would pay real money for this week \- not after the platform is built?"*

5. *"What are the 3 strongest competitors or alternatives \- including 'do nothing,' spreadsheets, agencies, and manual work? What do they do that I'd struggle to replicate?"*

6. *"What changed recently that makes this possible or urgent now?"*

7. *"What is the single biggest reason this fails?"*

If the AI only praises the idea, your prompt is too soft. If you can answer all seven with specifics, your idea is ready. If you can't, the hackathon is the place to sharpen it.

## **What investors actually fund in 2026**

## The bar has moved. Thousands of AI wrapper products launched in the last two years and investor appetite for thin wrappers has cratered. Sequoia calls it a "tale of two AIs" \- adoption is accelerating but most startups are still building wrappers.  Bessemer: vertical AI is bigger than vertical SaaS because it taps the labor line, not the IT line.

## Investors fund things that *only this team could build in this moment* \- proprietary data, embedded workflow depth, a real distribution wedge, or hardware/regulatory surface a wrapper cannot replicate. If your pitch could be rebuilt by a strong AI-native team in a sprint, you do not have a company.

## Sources: [**YC Requests for Startups**](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs), [**a16z Big Ideas 2026**](https://a16z.com/newsletter/big-ideas-2026-part-1/), [**Sequoia AI in 2026**](https://sequoiacap.com/article/ai-in-2026-the-tale-of-two-ais/).

1. **AI-native service companies** \- don't sell software, sell the service. Replace the accountant, the insurance broker, the compliance officer.

2. **SaaS challengers** \- AI collapsed the cost of building software by 10–100x. Go after products that seem invulnerable: ERPs, industrial control systems, chip design software, supply chain.

3. **Software for agents** \- every major software category needs rebuilding for AI agents as first-class users. APIs, MCPs, CLIs instead of buttons and dashboards.

4. **Voice AI agents** \- not chatbots, but voice agents that handle whole workflows: booking, intake, dispatch, customer service, clinical interactions.

5. **Physical-world AI and robotics** \- AI leaving the screen for factories, construction sites, supply chains, agriculture. Autonomous labs that close the loop from hypothesis to result.

6. **Defense and energy** \- counter-swarm defense, autonomous drones, electro-industrial infrastructure, edge computing.

7. **AI for science and medicine** \- personalized medicine from genome data, AI-native discovery engines for drugs and materials.

8. **Selling to the biggest companies in the world** \- F100 enterprises are actively looking for AI startups; YC companies are landing multi-million-dollar pilots in year one.

###   **Use investor taste as inspiration, not a checklist**

### Investor taste has shifted away from thin AI wrappers and toward companies with a real wedge: workflow ownership, proprietary data, distribution, regulated or physical-world complexity, or replacing expensive service labor. Use YC, a16z, Sequoia, Bessemer, and NFX as inspiration \- not as a shopping list. Do not build something because a trend report says the category is hot. Build because you understand a specific pain better than the market does.

## **How angels actually decide**

## Angels write checks in 15 minutes of conversation. No process, no model \- just whether the next two years of your life look like a venture-scale outcome. Your goal on June 6 is to earn those 15 minutes.

### **The decision order**

### At pre-seed, an angel's order is: **team → idea as evidence of team quality → wedge → deal**. Calacanis: *"At the seed stage, you are not investing in an idea. You are investing in a person's ability to figure things out."* Do not lead with the TAM slide. Lead with why *you* are the team that wins this market.

###  **What "team" actually means to an angel**

* **Founder-market fit.** Have you lived this problem? Do you carry unfair information?   
  Rachleff: *"The reason most startups fail is not because they can't build the product, but because they don't have founder-market fit."*

* **Speed of learning under pressure.** The founder who says "that's a good point, I haven't tested it \- here's how I'd test it Monday" beats the founder who defends.

* **Founder-distribution fit.** Can *you* reach buyers nobody else can? Naval: *"Distribution is a tax on every founder who didn't think about it on day one."*

* **Co-founder dynamic.** Clarity of roles, history, how you handle disagreement in front of them. Defensive correction \= red flag. Crisp respectful correction \= green.

###  **What angels disqualify in the first 60 seconds**

1. Vague "we're exploring AI in \[space\]" \- no decision, no wedge, no commitment.

2. CTO who cannot describe what they shipped *last week*.

3. Founder-idea mismatch \- a generalist pitching "AI for radiology workflow optimization" with no radiologist co-founder.

4. No wedge \- "we're going after the entire $50B legal market" with no first customer named.

5. "Give us money so we can quit our jobs" \- at pre-seed, angels back people already moving.

###  **Three moves that make an angel lean forward**

* **Be specific in the first 15 seconds.** Not "we're building an AI agent for healthcare." Instead: "we're the scheduling layer for ambulatory surgery centers in Germany that still run on Excel and fax." Specificity says *we know our buyer*.

* **Install founder-market fit in the first 30 seconds**, before the product slide. One sentence: *"I ran ops at one of these surgery centers and watched hours every week disappear into scheduling conflicts."*

* **Bring one piece of non-copyable evidence.** A customer screenshot. A demo nobody else could have built. A proprietary insight. Angels react to evidence of pull, not opinion.

## **Solo or with a co-founder?**

## Roughly **10% of YC-funded teams are solo.** Not because solo can't win \- Pieter Levels and Markus Frind (Plenty of Fish) did. It's because angels read solo as a recruiting signal: *if nobody great would join this person, why should I write a check?*

## If you're arriving solo, two extra goals:

1. **Find a co-founder before June 6\.** Join the Discord ([https://discord.gg/MCYGuUQ2vd](https://discord.gg/MCYGuUQ2vd)) and post in \#find-your-team. Even a temporary "for-the-day" co-founder gives a judge a window into how you operate alongside someone.

2. **Demonstrate recruiting velocity.** Name one specific person you're actively talking to \- by role and seniority. *"I'm in late conversations with a senior backend engineer from N26 joining as my technical co-founder in July."* That reframes solo from red flag to in-transition.

Do not pretend a loose chat is a co-founder. If you're recruiting someone, say it plainly: role, what they bring, why they might join. If you stay solo, make the project narrow enough that one person can execute it visibly and well.

## **Practice the live pitch**

## Everyone pitches live, so don't only prepare the deck \- prepare the first minute.

* **First sentence:** buyer \+ painful workflow \+ what you built.

* **Team edge:** why your team understands this problem better than a random team in the room.

* **Evidence:** one real signal, even if small.

* **Weakest point:** name the biggest risk yourself, and say what you'd test next.

Defensive answers are weaker than clear learning plans. Strong teams know what could kill the idea and what they'd do next.

## 

## **What strong looks like on June 6**

## A strong team usually has:

* one clear buyer

* one painful workflow

* a demo that makes the workflow feel easier

* a deck that avoids buzzwords

* one piece of evidence from a real person

* an honest view of what's still weak

Do not try to look like a mature startup in 12 hours. Try to look like a team that learns fast, builds fast, and knows where the real pain is. That's enough to earn a serious conversation.
